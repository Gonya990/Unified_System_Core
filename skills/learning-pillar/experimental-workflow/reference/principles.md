# Experimental Workflow - Scientific Method Principles

## The Scientific Method

**From Wikipedia, quoted in book**:
1. **Characterize**: Make an observation of the current state
2. **Hypothesize**: Create a description, a theory that may explain your observation
3. **Predict**: Make a prediction based on your hypothesis
4. **Experiment**: Test your prediction

> "When we organize our thinking this way and start to make progress on the basis of many small informal experiments, we begin to limit our risk of jumping to inappropriate conclusions and so end up doing a better job."

## Toyota Kata

**From Mike Rother's work, referenced in book**:

> "This is a simple, light-weight application of the scientific method. This should be obvious. This should be 'motherhood and apple pie,' but it is not what most people in most organizations do."

**Process**:
1. Understand current condition
2. Define target condition
3. Experiment toward target (one step)
4. Check and adjust
5. Repeat

## SpaceX Example

**From the book**: When switching from 4mm to 3mm stainless steel:
- Had all mathematical models
- Had tensile strength data  
- Had computer simulations

**Still** built physical prototypes and tested to destruction.

**Why?** "These models will certainly be wrong in some maybe esoteric, difficult-to-predict way."

## Margaret Hamilton's Focus on Failure

> "There was a fascination on my part with errors, a never ending pass-time of mine was what made a particular error, or class of errors, happen and how to prevent it in the future."

**Approach**: Try to DISPROVE hypotheses, not confirm them (Popperian falsification).

## Controlling Variables

> "If we start to think in terms of controlling the variables in our experiments so that we can achieve more consistency and reliability in our results, this leads us in the direction of more deterministic systems and code."

**Application**: Tests should be deterministic - same input → same output, every time.
